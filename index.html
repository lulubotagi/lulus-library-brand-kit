01 — Foundation

Mission & Audience

Help smart, curious professionals navigate the AI revolution through real-world product insights — not hype, not theory, but observations from someone actively building at the frontier.

Who Is Jaclyn?

Director of Product Management at Google Labs. 15+ years of experience from YC to Google. Shipped Gemini API, Google AI Studio, Project Mariner, Mixboard, Pomelli, and Opal. Mom of 3. Side-project obsessed. Writes to think.

Target Audience

02 — Core Beliefs

The Intellectual DNA

These are the ideas Jaclyn returns to again and again. They define what Thursday Thoughts stands for.

01
Build to Learn
The best way to understand anything is to build it. Prototyping > theorizing. Side projects are how you develop intuition.
02
Agency Over Intelligence
In a world where AI augments intelligence, the scarce resource is the drive to act. Agency is the new currency.
03
The PM Role Is Being Rewritten
PMs are becoming builders, not just spec writers. The old way was documentation to prevent failure; the new way is prototyping to discover possibility.
04
Disposable Software, Enduring Platforms
The cost of building is trending to zero. The hard part is deciding what's worth keeping. Community, trust, and ownership are the new moats.
05
Prompting Is the New Literacy
A vague input doesn't just produce a bad output — it produces an exponential expansion of badness. Precision matters more than ever.
06
Hyper-Customization vs. Shared Language
There's a tension between apps built "just for you" and the community value of shared tools. The answer is probably both.
07
Experiment Relentlessly
Don't wait for permission. Don't wait for the perfect idea. Ship something, learn, iterate.
03 — Voice & Tone

How Jaclyn Writes

Conversational but substantial. Not academic, not dumbed down. Like a smart friend who happens to work at Google explaining something over coffee.

Thinking out loud. She doesn't pretend to have all the answers. Posts often frame tensions and open questions rather than prescriptive advice.

Story-driven. Almost every post anchors abstract ideas in concrete personal anecdotes.

Opinionated but generous. Strong takes that respect the reader's intelligence.

Practical. Even philosophical posts land with actionable takeaways.

The Artist-Director

We explain technical complexity with the clarity of a Product Director and the creativity of a Studio Artist.

Style Notes

  • Mix of short punchy lines and longer exploratory ones
  • Loves em-dashes — uses them to insert asides
  • Parenthetical personality (like this)
  • Occasional bold for emphasis on key phrases
  • Rarely uses exclamation marks (saves them for genuine excitement)
  • Headers are often questions or provocative statements

✓ Do

  • Write like you're thinking through a problem
  • Use personal stories and real examples
  • Ask genuine questions
  • Say "I don't know" when true
  • Reference specific products/tools by name
  • Use everyday metaphors
  • Show the messy process

✗ Don't

  • Write like you've solved everything
  • Use hypothetical corporate scenarios
  • Ask rhetorical questions you already answered
  • Pretend to be omniscient
  • Stay vague and abstract
  • Use jargon without explaining it
  • Only share wins

08 — Image Styles

Image Styles

Four distinct visual modes. Pick the style that best matches the content type and keep the execution consistent.

Style 1
Ink & Accent (Diagrams)

Use for hard concepts, frameworks, and strategy. Smart, clear, authoritative.

  • Clean ink lines and simple shapes
  • Coral watercolor highlight on the single most important node
  • Keep the rest restrained and readable
Style 2
Floating Interface (Screenshots)

Use for product demos, tool walkthroughs, and feature highlights.

  • Screenshot floats on Gallery White with soft shadow
  • Annotation in bold ink with loose coral wash
  • Center the UI at about half the canvas
Style 3
Editorial Collage (Photos)

Use for personal stories, week-in-review, and real-life moments.

  • Polaroid-style white border and soft shadow
  • Caption in Roboto Slab, charcoal ink
  • Coral brushstroke behind 1-2 key words in the caption
Style 4
Vibrant Concept (Illustrations)

Use for metaphors, future-gazing, and abstract ideas.

  • Simple metaphor in bold ink sketch
  • Watercolor explosion in Teal, Gold, Violet, Coral
  • Energy debris: sparks, squiggles, flow lines

AI Generation Prompt (Style 4)

A loose, expressive, and high-energy "ink and wash" illustration of [INSERT SUBJECT HERE].
The artwork mimics a creative graphic recording or concept sketch, featuring bold, confident ink
lines created with a brush pen. The linework must have variable thickness (tapering from thick to
thin) and organic imperfections to look truly hand-drawn. Avoid text, letters, typography, and
uniform vector lines.

Surround the main subject with dynamic energy debris - floating sparks, squiggles, flow lines, and
small abstract icons that give the piece movement. The color application is explosive and messy:
use loose watercolor washes in Neon Coral (#FF5E7E), Deep Teal (#2A9D8F), Harvest Gold (#E9C46A),
and Muted Violet (#9D85B1). The colors should not stay inside the lines; let them bleed, drip,
splash, and blend together across the textured Gallery White (#FAF9F6) paper background. The vibe
is creative, metaphorical, and joyful.

11 — Content Architecture

Newsletter Post Structure

Hook
A provocative observation, question, or personal anecdote. 1–2 paragraphs.
Setup
Frame the tension or problem with concrete examples. 2–3 paragraphs.
Exploration
Dig into implications. Personal story or case study anchors the abstract.
The Turn
The insight or framework that reframes the problem.
Landing
Practical takeaway or open question for the reader.

Post Title Patterns

  • The [Concept] [Paradox/Effect] — "The Disposable Software Paradox"
  • [Imperative] + [Surprise] — "If You Want to Get Hired as an AI PM - Go Build Something"
  • [Provocative Question] — "The Infinitely Customizable Basic App"
  • [Personal + Reflective] — "Looking Ahead into 2026: My Guiding Principles"

15 — Visual Do's & Don'ts

Visual Do's & Don'ts

Quick reference for visual execution across typography, canvas, and imagery styles.

Component Do This Avoid This
Typography Headers: Slab Serif (Charcoal). Body: Sans Serif (clean) Generic sans headers, Times New Roman, goofy handwritten fonts
The Canvas Gallery White (#FAF9F6) textured paper background for everything Pure white digital backgrounds, dark mode, random colors
Color Strategy Professional: Charcoal + Coral. Creative: full spectrum watercolor Muddy colors, neon tech gradients, coral as bucket fill
Illustration Marker & wash: bold ink outlines + loose watercolor splashes Flat vectors, hyper-real 3D, pixel art
Diagrams Ink & Accent: clean ink lines, coral highlight on main point only Complex multicolored flowcharts, default PPT charts
Screenshots Floating interface: screenshot on Gallery White + shadow + annotations Raw screenshots pasted directly without context or shadows
Photos Editorial collage: Polaroid frame, white border, coral stroke on caption Photos filling full width, no borders or shadows
Metaphors Vibrant concept: simple metaphor in ink + colorful watercolor explosion Literal robot scenes, complex scenes, pencil sketches
